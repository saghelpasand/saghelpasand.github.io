About Me

Hello! I’m Samaneh Aghelpasand, an enthusiastic astronomer with a Master’s degree in Astronomy and Astrophysics from Alzahra University in Tehran. My journey into the cosmos began with a passion for understanding the universe, which led me to explore topics like the fascinating behaviors of water masers in star-forming regions. During my studies, I developed a neural network to identify exoplanets using data from the Kepler Space Telescope, which was both challenging and rewarding. I also completed my Bachelor's in Physics, where I delved into the electromagnetic detection of meteor signals—an experience that ignited my curiosity for the unknown. I love sharing my research at international workshops and conferences, where I connect with fellow astronomy enthusiasts and learn from experts in the field. Outside of my academic work, I’m an active member of the Astronomy Society of Iran, where I engage with a vibrant community of astronomers and contribute to our shared passion for the stars. When I’m not immersed in research, you can find me capturing the beauty of the night sky through photography or exploring new places. I believe that both science and art can illuminate our understanding of the universe, and I strive to blend these passions in my work.

Research

My current research projects encompass a diverse range of topics within observational astronomy. My B.Sc. thesis focused on the electromagnetic detection of ELF/VLF signals emitted by meteors under the supervision of Dr. Mahmoodreza Sharifian. For my M.Sc. thesis, I designed a Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) neural network to identify exoplanets from Kepler Space Telescope data, guided by Dr. Taghi Mirtorabi. In collaborative research, I contributed to the discovery of polarized water vapor megamaser emission in a molecular accretion disk, working alongside esteemed colleagues such as Prof. Jack Gallimore and Dr. Violette Impellizzeri, where we analyzed the proper motions of H2O maser emissions in NGC 1068 using VLBA data. Additionally, I participated in the ASPIRE Summer School Program at the University of Amsterdam, focusing on Fast Radio Bursts (FRBs) with Prof. J.W.T. Hessels and Dr. Ziggy Pleunis, where I explored radio polarimetry and fitting algorithms. Currently, I am involved in VLBA observations of the proper motions of water masers in the Cepheus A HW2 star-forming region, continuing my collaboration with Prof. Gallimore and Dr. Impellizzeri. These projects reflect my commitment to advancing our understanding of astrophysical phenomena through innovative methodologies and collaborative efforts.

Publications

  • The Discovery of Polarized Water Vapor Megamaser Emission in a Molecular Accretion Disk. In this pioneering study, we present the first linearly polarized H₂O maser emission detection in the extragalactic source NGC 1068. Our findings reveal that the electric polarization vectors are oriented perpendicularly to the filamentary structures in the molecular accretion disk, suggesting a complex interplay of magnetic fields.
  • Electromagnetic Detection of ELF/VLF Signals Emitted by Geminids 2017 Meteors. Skywatchers have long been intrigued by the radiant glow of meteors. Early reports indicate that sounds from these luminous meteors have been recorded, which is rare due to sound's slower speed compared to light. Astronomers suggest that ionized tails of meteors can generate electromagnetic waves in the ELF and VLF bands, causing nearby metal objects to vibrate and produce audible sounds, known as the Electrophonic effect. These waves travel at the speed of light, as confirmed by various measurements. A study detailed the detection of such signals during the 2017 Geminids meteor shower using a loop antenna and SuperSID monitor, effectively distinguishing these signals from local and natural noise. The research also discusses factors affecting data recording, highlighting an overlooked aspect of meteor observations and guiding future studies in this area.
